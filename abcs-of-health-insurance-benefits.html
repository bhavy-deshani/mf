ABCs of health insurance benefits: A practical guide

Almost all health insurance plans appear to be complex at first glance. Insurance companies may make tall promises. Not all of them may be untrue too. But just the way a policy document reads can be very overwhelming for the customer. The jargon and the amount of fine print that has to be gone through and understood can put you off. Also, invariably, when a claim is being made, the company may bring up legalese to disallow the claim, in full or in part. They may draw the customer’s attention to several of the terminologies that were used in the policy contract; this is stuff that the customer had actually signed up for.

Here's a simple, practical guide to help you understand the jargon and terminologies that are prevalent in the health insurance industry.

Premium: This is the amount that you would pay the health insurance company when purchasing your policy. This amount is dependant on the sum insured, the features of the policy, the age of the person insured and some other factors. A health insurance premium may be available for payment on a monthly, quarterly half-yearly or annual basis.

Sum insured: This is the amount that is covered by the health insurance policy. The insurance company is liable to pay this amount favoring the person insured as per the terms of the policy.

Waiting period: Almost all health insurance companies insist on a waiting period to be completed before the policy can be used. This waiting period may not be applicable for emergencies but elective surgeries and treatments will not be allowed during this period. The waiting period may vary based on the company issuing the policy, the policy itself and the coverage benefits.

Deductible: A deductible is a fixed amount that is payable by you at the time of A policy that has a deductible amount payable by the customer may come at a lower premium. So customers often choose such a policy. But in some cases the customers buy the policy unaware that it has a deductible clause. This causes confusing at the time of processing a claim. It is important for customers to therefore know upfront if their policy has a deductible clause or not.

Co-payment: The co-pay or co-payment clause in a health insurance policy refers to a percentage of the claim that the insured person (the customer) has to pay when a claim is being settled. This means if a claim for Rs.100 is being made, a percentage of that will be co-paid by the customer and the balance amount alone will be settled by the insurance company. This clause too must be read and clarified by the customer before buying the health insurance policy.

Add-on covers/riders: An add-on cover is an optional health insurance cover that customers can choose. This benefit will cover an additional illness that may show up as an emergency. An add-on cover may increase the premium value but protects the insured person against higher medical costs.

Automatic restoration: This is a benefit that health insurance companies offer. When a customer exhausts the value of the sum insured due to hospitalization, this amount is topped up by the company when the need for the next round of hospitalization arises. This benefit too comes at a higher premium.

Co-morbidities/pre-existing diseases: Patients with diabetes, hypertension, kidney ailments, cardiac conditions and such other health issues are considered to be carrying high risk factors according to insurance companies. Such patients are offered insurance plans with a higher premium.

Critical illnesses: Any life-threatening ailment falls under this category. This category is covered by separate health insurance plans. Or customers may opt for add-ons to cover the treatment costs for such ailments.

No-claim bonus: When customers own a policy but do not make a claim during the policy year they become eligible for a no-claim bonus. This is usually an amount that is equivalent to up to 100% the sum insured. This bonus may vary based on policy plans and insurance companies.

Network hospitals: Health insurance companies work with a wide network of hospitals. Customers of an insurance company may choose to visit a hospital that is within the company’s network to get the best benefits of their policies. Almost all hospitals in an insurer’s network offer cashless services to patients. This means that the insurer will settle the patient’s bills directly with the hospital.

Cashless claims: This is a policy benefit feature. Most health insurance policy today offer this benefit. This means that the insurer will settle the patient’s bills directly with the hospital in the insurer’s network.

Exclusions: These are the terms of the policy under which a health insurance claim can be rejected by the company. These terms are explicitly mentioned in the policy document.

Inclusions: These are coverage benefits that are included in the policy. The inclusions may include ambulance charges, surgery costs, rooms bills at hospitals and other treatment-related expenses. This list is also available in the policy document.

The bottom line

Read the policy document carefully to understand what is covered and what is not. Review, clarify, understand and then sign up for your health insurance policy.
