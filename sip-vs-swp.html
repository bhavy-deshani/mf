Key Difference Between SIP vs SWP

Before we understand these terms and the key differences between them, we need to define what an investment is. Regulating one’s cashflow to get fruitful returns in the future is known as investment. Choosing an investment method that aligns with your financial goal is an art.

Once you have your financial goal outlined clearly, and have planned your financial transactions well, you need to choose a systematic financial approach to achieve your goal.

Mutual funds are a popular investment option. Systematic Investment Plan (SIP) and Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP) are two commonly used terms in mutual funds. In an SIP, investors invest a fixed sum of money regularly into mutual fund schemes. While an SWP allows investors to withdraw a fixed sum of money at regular intervals from their investments. The two acronyms are complete opposites of each other. Understanding the SIP Vs SWP debate is important for any investor. While in SIP, an investor can benefit from rupee cost averaging, SWPs are ideal for generating income.

What is Systematic Investment Plan (SIP)?

It is a strategic approach to investment in a mutual fund where you invest a fixed amount of money into a scheme of your choice at regular intervals. You can choose an interval based on your comfort like daily, weekly, monthly, or quarterly. An SIP is not only affordable but offers advantages like compounding interest, rupee cost averaging, and flexibility. It also instils a habit of disciplined saving and investing in a person.

SIPs work on a simple principle that you invest in mutual funds with a small and regular contribution and build a corpus over time. An SIP can be started with a nominal sum of INR 500! Therefore, SIPs gain benefits of market-linked returns with regular and manageable contributions instead of hefty lump sum deposits that are not feasible for everyone. SIPs are perfect for long-term wealth creation but can also be used to meet short-term goals.

Every mutual fund scheme has a Net Asset Value (NAV): it is basically the price of one of its units. The NAV is updated daily based on the performance of the mutual fund in the market. Every time you make a contribution to the mutual fund, you are buying units at that day’s unit price.

The benefit on an SIP is their flexibility. You can easily change the contribution amount or adjust the frequency of your investments depending on your situation or financial goal. In case you face a financial emergency, an SIP even offers the option to temporarily pause your contributions without cancelling the plan.

What is a Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP)?

An SWP is the exact opposite of an SIP. As already explained, in a SIP, you are putting a fixed amount regularly into a mutual fund of your choice. On the other hand, in a SWP, you are withdrawing a fixed amount regularly from your mutual fund investment. This means that you are redeeming the units of your mutual fund at the prevailing Net Asset Value (NAV).

So, in a SWP, the amount you receive or withdraw, is dependent on the current NAV on the day of withdrawal. With each withdrawal, the number of units in your mutual fund goes down till it reaches zero. Till that happens, the remaining balance will continue to grow and generate funds based on the market performance of the fund. SWPs are ideal for individuals who have amassed a large corpus of funds and are looking to receive a steady stream of income. This type of investment is preferred by retirees as the SWP allows them to meet their financial needs without liquidating their entire investment at once. Just as an SIP allows you to decide how much you want to invest and how often (weekly, quarterly or monthly), an SWP allows you to decide how much you want to withdraw and how frequently.

Key difference between SIP and SWP

Understanding the differences between SIP and SWP is crucial for investors who are beginning and stepping into the mutual fund arena for the first time. The primary difference between the two is that SIP focuses on wealth accumulation while SWP focuses on income generation from existing investments. Now let us look at the differences in detail.

1. Benefits

SIPs require regular contributions. This cultivates a regular saving and investment habit in a person. SIP investments are spread over a long period of time and therefore minimise the impact of market volatility. This means that you make your investments irrespective of the market behaviour. Additionally, you enjoy the cost of rupee cost averaging. This means that you purchase more MF units when the market is down and fewer units when the market is up. This lowers the average cost of each unit. SIP is a great tool for building long-term wealth.

In a SWP on the other hand, you have an investment that can be redeemed at regular intervals and gives you a regular source of income. You can customize the amount to be withdrawn as well as the frequency of withdrawals depending on your requirements.

2. Suitability

The suitability of either SIP or SWP depends on what the investor wants. An SIP is suitable for those seeking long-term wealth creation through regular, manageable contributions. An SIP investor is willing to save and invest regularly in a market-linked product. Essentially, an SIP is suitable for a small investor who finds it difficult to invest lump sum amounts in one go.

SWPs on the other hand are tailored for investors who seek a steady and reliable source of income. Investors who have a lump sum of money like a retiree for example, can invest in a SWP and ensure a steady income flow to cover monthly obligations and expenses.

3. How does it work?

The operations of SIPs are relatively simple. An investor contributes a fixed sum at regular intervals regardless of market conditions. This contribution is used to but mutual fund units. The number of units purchased will depend of the NAV of the mutual fund units. When the market is low, more units are purchased and when the market is high, fewer units are purchased.

In a SWP on the other hand, the investor puts in a large corpus into a mutual fund scheme. Based on their instructions, the fund house redeems a certain amount of money at regular intervals. In order to meet your requirement, the fund house sells the mutual fund units from your portfolio at the prevailing NAV.

4. Tax implication

SIPs are investment avenues and therefore do not attract taxes. However, when you redeem you SIP investment, taxes will be applicable. Certain mutual funds offer a tax deduction on their SIP investments. Equity Linked Saving Schemes (ELSS) are

tax-saving equity mutual funds.

SWPs on the other hand are strictly for withdrawals and hence attract taxes. There is no Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) in a SWP but a capital gains tax is applicable based on the scheme and holding period of the funds.

In a nutshell, the key differences are:

• An SIP allows you to systematically grow your investment, while an SWP is about systematically withdrawing from funds that you have already invested.

• In a SIP, you are regularly investing a fixed amount to grow your money over a period of time, while a SWP is ideal for income generation.

Conclusion

Both SIP and SWP are strategic and systematic methods by which you can easily invest and withdraw from mutual funds. Both have their own benefits for investors. SIPs and SWPs have different purposes and objectives. Therefore, you must choose one based on your requirements. SIPs are designed to help investors achieve their financial goals and build long-term wealth. SWPs are focused on generating a stable and consistent income stream for investors. You must choose between them based on your financial goals, investment objectives and cash flow needs.
