All about integrated financial planning

Integrated financial planning is not just a new management idea. It is also not just another method or tool to make sense of your organization’s financials. It is sound business strategy.

With integrated financial planning you secure the financial future of your company by strategically tying it up with your organizational vision. This represents a significant shift in the way you approach financial planning. It used to be a very closed, departmental affair with essentially the finance team alone deciding what every other department must be doing to achieve business results. This included budgeting and sanctioning funds for each department by the finance team. But with integrated finance planning, the approach has turned collaborative. Every department is consulted, every department shares data and every department is engaged with the overall business plan of the company. Nobody is left out.

The old order

Typically, the finance team gathers data – after pushing, jostling and pleading – from every other department. Some of that data is incomplete or even erroneous at times. Then the finance team spends several long hours estimating the outlook for the business and in building the business plan. When this plan is finally shared with the other departments there is no buy-in from anyone. In fact, there is a lot of confusion and pushback. From operations to sales to marketing to HR to purchase to IT, no one is willing to believe that the revenue and earnings projections are achievable. So, basically, the company just stumbles on, into yet another business year.

The key benefit of integrated financial planning

It is a definitive game changer. When the various departments collaborate with the finance team to develop the next year’s business plan, two things are certain:

1. There are no data siloes. People fill information and data gaps to make sure that the data they share integrates to complete the whole, big picture for the company.

2. There is ownership of the business plan for each department because the plan has been contributed to by each of them. Basically, collaboration results in shared ownership.

Importantly, the business plan looks real to every department. Because they have co-created it. They know that they have committed to a financial goal. And they know they have to achieve it. Unless there are any unforeseen market-related or other circumstances, no department has the option of not delivering on the numbers that they have promised.

Truly integrated

The emphasis is on the word integrated in this financial planning approach. It involves integration at three levels in the company:

• Through software: This means the data being used for financial planning integrates with the software of the enterprise. This leads to data accuracy and offer seamless access to all important function heads and leaders across the company.

• Through collaboration among leaders: When the leadership in a company is engaged closely with business planning, there is greater collaboration and therefore greater ownership. This enhances the chances of consistent high performance.

• Through offering continuous improvements: When everyone is involved with developing and reviewing the business plan, then quarterly and mid-year reviews allow for continuous improvements seamlessly.

In other words, integrated financial planning fully pan-organizational data using the enterprise’s software, it brings the leadership together to build and co-own the business plan and allows for reviews and improvements on a continuous basis. How to practice integrated financial planning?

At a software level

You must begin with integration at a software level. Accurate financial data is key to building a powerful and efficient business plan. The IT team has to lead this part of the process. They must invest time collecting, verifying and cleaning up all the data from all the departments. The data sets that emerge from this exercise must then be one that the enterprise’s software will crunch and deploy in the business plan.

The focus is to make your organization’s financial planning simpler and error-free. If the company is using different software platforms, the IT team must look for a viable software solution that helps integrate these diverse platforms and all the associated data that they hold.

The final test of the success of this exercise is when all key department leaders and function heads can seamlessly access data, and can certify that the data is genuinely free of any error.

At a leadership level

Due to the way organizations have typically evolved over time, department heads function in silos. They protect their teams and their prefer to not allow too much access to their departments from the rest of the organization.

For integrated financial planning to be successful, this siloed approach must change.

Start by inviting department heads to share what their business goals for the year are. Which is, what are they planning to achieve? How much money and resources are they planning to spend to achieve these goals? And ask them to justify how this expenditure will contribute to the organization’s revenues and profits.

The finance team may then use all this information to build a plan that may be sent back to the department heads for their inputs and sign-offs. Once there is a complete buy-in, the business plan is ready for launch.

At an ongoing improvement level

This is the most important aspect of integrated financial planning. Just as in sports, when a team’s performance needs to be reviewed and improved in the middle of a game, so it is in business too. The integrated financial planning approach allows for dynamic, real-time reviews and continuous improvements. This makes the organization agile and nimble-footed. An organization that can adapt to market developments quickly always has an advantage over its competitors.

These reviews may essentially focus on the following aspects:

• Why were the numbers achieved?

• Why were they not achieved?

• What were the external (market) factors responsible for our performance?

• What were the internal factors responsible for our performance?

• How to maximize and sustain what worked for us and minimize and eliminate what isn’t working for us?

Conclusion

Integrated financial planning is a very powerful idea. No organization can afford to ignore it. The value created with this approach is immense:

• An accurate business plan that is co-created and co-owned by your entire leadership team.

• A practical, authentic budget that can be counted to reliably deliver high

performance.

• When team members collaborate to create a plan, they know what is expected of them to make that plan successful.
